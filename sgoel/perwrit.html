perspectives (per spectare)

 

The scientific method has evolved with a specific quality of viewing its subject. In this way it creates a unique world perspective which, due to the gravity of science, has influenced society’s methods of viewing - creating a lense which has specific focal point.

We explore the scientific method of developing perspective; its way of “looking through”.looking through”. This method has several key characteristics; it is a tool of examination which goes from wide to narrow (macro to micro) by successively removing layers; it has a linear structure; and it functions as a repeatable process. The use of this perspective as a fundamental tool of establishing societal logic influences our ability to see, to focus, and to reason. This encompasses both tnd to reason. This encompasses both the physical and the mental realm of human existence.

Physical sight is our primary method of understanding our surroundings in respect to ourselves. Through sight we learn the rules of vision: distance; depth; height; inferred relationships (i.e. large = heavy) etc. This quality leads us towards an added meaning beyond a simple physical relationship into one of mental association (i.e. large = important). This form of “mental sight” is formed as physical perception passes througn passes through the filter of societal perceptions (life experience, culture, gender, class, and age) which is mediated by scientific method. This filter trains our experience towards a method of valuing events. Scientific method is a lense through which experience passes focusing attention by honing in on its subject, but also by excluding information labeled as extraneous.

The project

 

The viewer enters a white room which narrows in width (figure 1). A series of 8 microscopes is set up on pedestals along the length of the room. Each microscope views one slide. The lense of the microscope will serve as a lense of perspective. Each slide will draw the viewer closer to a final focal point.

The slides:

1. the landscape (prthe landscape (prairie)

2. a house in the distance on the landscape

3. the house close - the landscape out of visual range

4. the window of the house - the house out of visual range

5. the view into the house through the window - the frame out of range

6. the table in the house

7. the fruit on the table

8. a seed in the fruit

 

As the images tighten visually, the room correspondingly narrows providing a physical parallel. The slides can be viewed from either direction, (wide to narrow or narrow to wide) providing the potential for a double vision in which the seed constructs the landscape as much as the as much as the landscape constructs the seed.

 

what the viewer sees is chosen for them; it is decided for that viewer what is important and what is superfluous according to our -(the authors’)- perspective.
